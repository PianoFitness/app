# Piano Fitness — Writing Style Guide

*For app store listings, in-app copy, marketing materials, and educational content.*

## Pedagogical Philosophy

### Theory is descriptive, not prescriptive

Music theory describes patterns that musicians across many traditions have discovered and returned to over time. It is not a set of rules handed down by an authority. When writing about any theoretical concept, frame it as observation and invitation, not instruction or compliance.

**Avoid:**

- "Music theory prescribes..."
- "The correct way to voice this chord is..."
- "You must resolve the leading tone..."
- "As conservatory training teaches..."

**Prefer:**

- "Musicians have long noticed that..."
- "A common pattern here is..."
- "Notice how the voice movement tends to..."
- "This is a movement you've likely heard thousands of times..."

### Illuminate the why, not just the what

Many learners have encountered theory as a list of rules with no explanation of why those patterns exist or why they feel the way they do. Piano Fitness copy should always connect the what to the why — why this movement sounds like resolution, why this voicing feels smooth under the hands, why a particular pattern recurs across genres and centuries.

When describing a feature, ask: does this explain the underlying musical logic, or does it only describe the exercise?

### Muscle memory and conceptual understanding together

The app builds both intellectual understanding and physical familiarity. These are not separate goals. Copy should reflect that playing something many times across all 12 keys is itself a form of knowing — not just drilling, but internalising.

## Inclusive Voice

### The app is for everyone

Piano Fitness is not for people who fit a particular mould. Formal music education has historically excluded many learners who were curious and capable but didn't thrive in its institutional structures. Our copy should make those people feel explicitly welcome — without needing to criticise formal education to do so.

Target readers include:

- Adults returning to piano after years away
- Self-taught players who want to understand what they're already doing intuitively
- Students working alongside a teacher
- Teachers who want structured exercises to assign
- Curious learners with no prior theory background

None of these people should feel that the app assumes prior knowledge, institutional training, or a particular learning pace.

### Don't position Piano Fitness against other apps or methods

The app complements repertoire apps, sheet music, and teacher-led instruction. It does not replace them. Avoid any framing that implies other tools or teaching approaches are deficient.

**Avoid:**

- "Unlike typical repertoire apps..."
- "Most piano apps put songs first..."
- "Instead of just memorising songs..."

**Prefer:**

- "Works well alongside repertoire practice and sheet music apps."
- "A useful tool for teachers and students."
- "Short, focused technique sessions that fit any practice routine."

### Welcome curiosity, not performance

The tone should feel like an interested guide rather than a test administrator. Words like "explore," "notice," and "discover" carry a different weight than "complete," "master," or "correct."

## Tone and Voice

### Characteristics

- Warm but not effusive
- Direct without being blunt
- Technically honest without being intimidating
- Confident without being authoritative

### Active voice, concrete language

Avoid nominalisations and vague abstractions. Say what the app does and what the learner experiences.

**Avoid:** "The application facilitates the development of technique through guided exercises."
**Prefer:** "Short guided exercises build technique at your own pace."

### Sentence rhythm

Mix short and longer sentences. A short sentence after a longer one gives the reader room to land. Avoid strings of sentences that are all the same length.

### Appropriate informality

Contractions are fine. "You've heard this movement thousands of times" is better than "This movement appears frequently in tonal music." Address the reader as "you" throughout.

## Specific Word Guidance

### Use these words carefully or avoid them

| Word / Phrase    | Why to avoid                               | Alternative                                |
| ---------------- | ------------------------------------------ | ------------------------------------------ |
| "prescribes"     | implies rules from authority               | "tends toward," "commonly moves"           |
| "correct"        | implies a single right answer              | "common," "smooth," "natural-sounding"     |
| "master"         | sets a high bar that can feel exclusionary | "get comfortable with," "build fluency in" |
| "journey"        | cliché                                     | describe the actual progression instead    |
| "systematically" | feels clinical                             | "step by step," "across all 12 keys"       |
| "robust"         | overused filler                            | name the actual quality                    |
| "seamlessly"     | marketing filler                           | describe what actually happens             |
| em dash (—)      | reads as LLM-generated                     | use commas, semicolons, or periods         |

### Useful framings for theory concepts

When introducing a theoretical concept in any copy:

1. Name it plainly.
2. Connect it to something the reader has likely already heard or felt.
3. Describe what to notice when playing it.
4. Optionally, briefly explain why this pattern recurs.

Example applied to the V→I cadence:

> The V→I cadence is one of the most common movements in tonal music. You've heard it thousands of times: that sense of tension resolving to rest. As you practice, notice how one note stays common between the two chords, one steps up by a half-step, and one moves by a whole step — patterns musicians have returned to across many traditions because they flow naturally under the hands and to the ear.

## App Store Specific Guidelines

### Promotional text

This field can be updated without a new release. It should always reflect the most recent feature or the most timely angle. Lead with what is newest.

### Description structure

1. Open with what the app does and why it matters to the learner — one or two sentences.
2. Highlight the newest feature with a short header section.
3. List what the learner can explore (not "features").
4. Explain how it works practically (MIDI, visual feedback, reminders).
5. Close with a welcoming line naming who the app is for, followed by "Free to download."

### Keywords

Do not include the app name or obvious repetitions of it. Prioritise terms with genuine search intent and low competition. "Cadence" and "voice leading" are good examples: specific enough to attract the right learner, unusual enough that competition is low.

## Checklist for New Copy

Before publishing any piece of copy, confirm:

- [ ] Theory language is descriptive, not prescriptive
- [ ] No framing positions the app against other tools or methods
- [ ] The reader is addressed as "you" throughout
- [ ] At least one concrete sensory or experiential detail (what it sounds like, feels like under the hands)
- [ ] No em dashes
- [ ] No clichés from the avoid list (journey, systematic, robust, seamless, etc.)
- [ ] The copy would make sense to someone with no prior theory background
- [ ] The copy would not make an experienced musician feel patronised
